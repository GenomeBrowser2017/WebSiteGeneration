Introduction

Salmonella enterica subsp. enterica serovar Heidelberg is a foodborne pathogen that can be transferred from animals to humans and can also cause a dangerous bacterial infection if it comes in contact with a human host. Even though it is rather dangerous, it is still fairly common to see the outbreaks and spread of this bacterium through contaminated poultry meat circulating around the market, especially in North America. S. Heidelberg is the second most common serovar isolated from humans in the U.S., and accounts for 8.6% of ceftriaxone-resistant Salmonella isolated from humans in the U.S. The recent 2013 outbreak, linked to raw chicken, was caused by a multidrug resistant S. Heidelberg strain (resistant to ampicillin, chloramphenicol, gentamicin, kanamycin, streptomycin, sulfisoxazole, and tetracycline).

Outbreak isolates are clusters of three or more isolates that are reported within 60 days and have the same PFGE pattern along with epidemiological links like location and food source. This browser was given biological samples from year 2013 from the Center for Disease Control in order to illuminate some information on these potentially dangerous outbreaks. These particular outbreak isolates are of interest to the CDC because they had a very short incubation period of ~3 hours compared to the standard ~72 hour incubation period.

The goal of this browser is to be able to identify and display pertinent information in regards to the full genome of 24 strains of S. Heidelberg in order to shed light on their pathogenicity and virulence so as to further facilitate steps to limit, contain and extinguish harmful cases towards humans.
